## English

This page does not ask you to assume moral duties,  
nor does it call for political activism.

It explains a simple point:

> **If a system systematically prevents most people  
> from improving their well-being,  
> then even the most rational and diligent individuals  
> will struggle to sustain their own happiness over time.**

---

## I. Well-being is personal, but not isolated

Well-being is experienced by individuals.  
It cannot be standardized or delegated.

But it does not occur in a vacuum.

Individual choices always operate within conditions such as:

- Stability of property  
- Predictability of rules  
- Enforceability of contracts  
- Distribution of risk  

These conditions are institutional, not personal.

---

## II. Institutions are constraints, not ideals

A system does not need to be malicious  
to produce harmful outcomes.

If it consistently generates:

- Higher costs for rule-followers  
- Risk transfer onto individuals  
- Asymmetric consequences  
- Frequent, unpredictable rule changes  

then rational choices become distorted.

In such environments,  
well-being becomes accidental rather than earned.

---

## III. Why â€œfocusing only on oneselfâ€ fails

Many attempt a strategy of withdrawal:

> â€œI will manage my own life  
> and avoid public concerns.â€

This may work temporarily,  
but fails in the long run because:

- Systemic risks cannot be fully hedged individually  
- Rule changes affect everyone simultaneously  
- Structural errors erode personal gains repeatedly  

Under persistent mismatch,  
the more rational one is,  
the sooner exhaustion appears.

---

## IV. From moral duty to rational incentive

This project does not claim  
that individuals are morally obligated to reform society.

It makes a calmer claim:

> **Caring about institutional correctness  
> is not moral heroism,  
> but long-term rational self-protection.**

Thus, individuals who value their own well-being  
naturally develop incentives to:

- Learn which systems reduce systemic harm  
- Promote ideas that improve resilience  
- Help build and maintain minimal shared frameworks  

This is not idealism,  
but risk management.

---

## V. Why learning, promoting, building, and maintaining?

Because institutions do not sustain themselves.

Even good systems decay without:

- Understanding  
- Shared recognition  
- Maintenance  
- Correction mechanisms  

Participation can take many forms:

- Understanding and articulation  
- Choice and exit  
- Small-scale cooperation  
- Rule-consistent practice  

**No single method is required.**

---

## VI. Boundary clarification

This page does not endorse:

- Coercion of others  
- Sacrifice of individuals for abstract goals  
- Violent or confrontational change  

On the contrary:

> **Any attempt to improve systems  
> by damaging individual well-being  
> has already failed its purpose.**

---

## Summary

Well-being is personal,  
but depends on an environment that does not systematically harm it.

Therefore:

> **Individuals seeking to improve their own lives  
> have rational incentives to learn, promote, build, and maintain  
> ideas, shared understandings, and constitutional frameworks  
> that allow most people to enhance their well-being freely.**

This is not an added burden,  
but a clarification of conditions.
